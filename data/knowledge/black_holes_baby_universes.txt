BLACK HOLES AND BABY UNIVERSES AND OTHER ESSAYS (1993)
Stephen W. Hawking

OVERVIEW

"Black Holes and Baby Universes and Other Essays" was published in 1993 by Bantam Books. Unlike Hawking's other popular books, which are unified around a single narrative or theme, this is a collection of essays and lectures written between 1976 and 1992. It offers a more personal, varied, and sometimes autobiographical window into Hawking's life and thought than his other popular works.

The book was accompanied by an audio cassette recording of Hawking reading from it — one of the first times the public could hear his synthesised voice reading his own words. It was a commercial success, though not on the scale of A Brief History of Time.

CONTENTS

1. Childhood — A personal essay in which Hawking reflects on his early life in Oxford and St Albans, his schooling, and his discovery of his love for mathematics and physics. He describes himself as an unremarkable student who was fascinated by how things worked. He discusses his family's eccentricities and his first encounters with science.

2. Oxford and Cambridge — Hawking describes his undergraduate years at Oxford, where he found the work unstimulating and spent more time rowing than studying. He reflects on being diagnosed with motor neurone disease (ALS) in 1963, aged 21, and how this paradoxically gave him a sense of purpose. "Before my condition had been diagnosed, I had been very bored with life. There had not seemed to be anything worth doing." He discusses moving to Cambridge for his PhD under Dennis Sciama and beginning his work on cosmology.

3. My Experience with Motor Neurone Disease — A frank and candid account of living with ALS. Hawking describes the progression of the disease, the gradual loss of physical function, and the adaptations he made — from using a wheelchair to losing his voice after a tracheotomy in 1985, and subsequently communicating through a speech synthesiser. He writes with characteristic dry humour and rejects self-pity entirely.

4. Public Attitudes to Science — Hawking discusses the relationship between science and the public, arguing that scientists have an obligation to communicate their work clearly and that the public has a right to engage with scientific questions. He expresses frustration with the way science is often portrayed as alien and incomprehensible.

5. A Brief History of A Brief History — Hawking recounts how he came to write A Brief History of Time: originally intended as a textbook but transformed into a popular account at the suggestion of his agent. He discusses the book's extraordinary commercial success and what it revealed about public appetite for big scientific ideas.

6. My Position — A discussion of Hawking's relationship with religion and his views on God. He describes himself as a scientist who does not invoke God as an explanation for natural phenomena, but who uses the word "God" in the same metaphorical sense as Einstein — to refer to the laws of nature. He is agnostic rather than dogmatically atheist in this essay, more cautious than his later works.

7. Baby Universes — The title essay. Hawking presents his hypothesis that black holes might give birth to "baby universes" — new, separate spacetime regions that pinch off from our universe and expand into universes of their own. Information that falls into a black hole might not be lost but instead might pass into a baby universe. This was Hawking's attempt to resolve the information paradox while preserving quantum mechanics.

8. The No-Boundary Proposal — An accessible account of the Hartle-Hawking state: the proposal that the universe has no boundary or beginning in imaginary time. Time is rounded off near the Big Bang, like the South Pole of a sphere, so there is no "edge" and no need to specify boundary conditions.

9. Is the End in Sight for Theoretical Physics? — A lecture Hawking gave as his inaugural address as Lucasian Professor of Mathematics at Cambridge (the same chair once held by Newton and Dirac). He argues that a complete unified theory of physics might be within reach by the end of the twentieth century, possibly in the form of N=8 supergravity. (This optimism proved premature.) He discusses what it would mean to have a final theory.

10. Do Wormholes Connect Different Universes? — Hawking discusses the physics of wormholes: hypothetical tunnels through spacetime connecting distant regions or even different universes. He considers whether wormholes could be used for time travel and why quantum effects probably prevent their use.

11. The Quantum Mechanics of Black Holes — A more technical essay explaining Hawking radiation and the thermodynamics of black holes, made accessible for a general reader.

12. Is Time Travel Possible? — Hawking presents arguments for and against time travel, culminating in his Chronology Protection Conjecture.

13. The Future of the Universe — Hawking speculates about the long-term fate of the universe, life, and intelligence, drawing on thermodynamics and cosmology.

14. How to Build Wormholes: A Beginner's Guide — A lighter piece in which Hawking half-seriously imagines how one might construct a wormhole or time machine, illustrating the practical and theoretical difficulties.

HAWKING'S OWN WORDS

"However difficult life may seem, there is always something you can do and succeed at. It matters that you don't just give up."

"I'm not afraid of death, but I'm in no hurry to die. I have so much I want to do first."

"The greatest enemy of knowledge is not ignorance; it is the illusion of knowledge."

"Although I cannot move and I have to speak through a computer, in my mind I am free."

"Science is not only a disciple of reason but, also, one of romance and passion."

SIGNIFICANCE

This collection is valuable because it provides the most personal and autobiographical account Hawking ever published during his lifetime. It shows the man behind the physics — his humour, his defiance of his disability, his philosophical caution, and his genuine wonder at the universe. The title essay on baby universes also represents an important (if ultimately unsuccessful) attempt to resolve the black hole information paradox, which Hawking later solved differently in 2004.
