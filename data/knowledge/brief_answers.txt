BRIEF ANSWERS TO THE BIG QUESTIONS (2018)
Stephen W. Hawking
Published posthumously by John Murray / Bantam Books

OVERVIEW

"Brief Answers to the Big Questions" was Stephen Hawking's final book, assembled from his unfinished manuscripts, essays, and notes by his family and colleagues after his death on 14 March 2018. It was published in October 2018. The book represents Hawking's definitive public statement on the ten questions he was asked most often throughout his life — not physics questions, but big human questions about existence, meaning, and the future. It is part science, part philosophy, and part urgent warning to humanity.

The book is dedicated: "To the people who will not give up."

THE TEN QUESTIONS AND HAWKING'S ANSWERS

1. IS THERE A GOD?
Hawking's answer: No. He argues that before science, it was natural to believe a personal God created and governed the universe. But science now provides a more convincing explanation. The laws of nature — particularly gravity — allow the universe to create itself spontaneously from nothing. There is no need to invoke a creator. "You can define God as the embodiment of the laws of nature. However, this is not what most people would mean by God. They mean a human-like being, with whom one can have a personal relationship. When you look at the vast size of the universe and how insignificant and accidental human life is in it, that seems most implausible." He uses his no-boundary proposal (with James Hartle) to argue that asking what happened before the Big Bang is meaningless — time itself began at the Big Bang, so there was no "before" in which a creator could have acted.

2. HOW DID IT ALL BEGIN?
The universe began with the Big Bang — an expansion from a state of infinite density about 13.8 billion years ago. Time itself began at the Big Bang. Hawking argues the universe is governed by laws of science that hold everywhere, at all times, without exception. The initial conditions of the universe can be calculated using the no-boundary wave function: the universe created itself from nothing, with no initial singularity, no outside cause. "The universe itself, in all its mind-boggling vastness and complexity, could simply have popped into existence without violating the known laws of nature."

3. IS THERE OTHER INTELLIGENT LIFE IN THE UNIVERSE?
Almost certainly. The universe contains hundreds of billions of galaxies, each with hundreds of billions of stars. It is statistically inconceivable that Earth is the only place where life arose. However, Hawking distinguished between primitive life (very common) and intelligent life (extremely rare). He is cautious about the Fermi paradox — the fact that we have heard nothing from intelligent aliens despite the universe's age — and interprets this as possibly meaning intelligent civilisations tend to destroy themselves shortly after developing advanced technology. Most urgently: "I think we should be wary of answering back. Meeting an advanced civilisation could be like the Native Americans encountering Columbus. That didn't turn out so well." He strongly advised against actively broadcasting our location.

4. CAN WE PREDICT THE FUTURE?
In principle, the laws of physics are deterministic — given the exact state of the universe now, the future is determined. Laplace believed that a sufficiently powerful intellect could predict everything. However, two things prevent this in practice. First, chaos theory: tiny uncertainties in initial conditions grow exponentially, making long-range prediction impossible even for classical systems (the butterfly effect). Second, quantum mechanics introduces fundamental, irreducible uncertainty. We cannot know both the position and velocity of a particle with perfect precision. So prediction has limits that are not merely practical but fundamental. "The future is not completely determined by the past, even in principle, in quantum mechanics."

5. WHAT IS INSIDE A BLACK HOLE?
At the centre of a black hole is a singularity — a point of infinite density where the known laws of physics break down. Nothing, not even light, can escape from inside a black hole once past the event horizon. However, Hawking's discovery (Hawking Radiation) shows that black holes are not completely black: they slowly emit thermal radiation due to quantum effects near the event horizon, eventually evaporating completely. What happens to the information about the matter that fell in remains one of the deepest unsolved problems in theoretical physics. Hawking's final position (after conceding his earlier bet in 2004) is that information is not destroyed but preserved, encoded in subtle ways in the radiation — possibly in the "soft hair" of quantum excitations on the horizon, as he argued in his 2016 paper with Perry and Strominger.

6. IS TIME TRAVEL POSSIBLE?
General relativity permits "closed timelike curves" — mathematical paths through spacetime that return to their own past. Wormholes could in principle connect different times. However, Hawking proposed the Chronology Protection Conjecture: quantum effects destroy any time machine before it can be used, keeping the universe safe for historians. He "proved" this experimentally by hosting a party for time travellers: he prepared a party with champagne, balloons, and hors d'oeuvres, then only after the party was over sent out invitations saying "Come to my party." No one came. As he noted: "I sat there a long time, but no one came. What a shame. I had champagne on ice." However, time travel to the FUTURE is real and well tested — clocks run slower in strong gravity or at high speed (gravitational time dilation and special relativistic time dilation).

7. WILL WE SURVIVE ON EARTH?
This chapter is Hawking's most urgent. He lists the threats to humanity's survival on Earth: climate change, the risk of nuclear war, epidemic diseases, asteroid impacts, and the potential misalignment of artificial intelligence. He is deeply concerned about all of these. His conclusion: humanity must become a multi-planetary species within the next few centuries or risk extinction. "I don't think the human race will survive the next thousand years unless we spread into space. There are too many accidents that can befall life on a single planet." He supports colonising the Moon, Mars, and eventually other star systems.

8. SHOULD WE COLONISE SPACE?
Yes — it is not optional, it is a necessity. Hawking argues that spreading into space is the long-term insurance policy for the human species. He supports the development of new propulsion technologies, including the Breakthrough Starshot project (which he backed shortly before his death) — a proposal to send tiny laser-propelled spacecraft to Alpha Centauri at 20% of the speed of light, reaching it in about 20 years. He is optimistic about human ingenuity: "I don't think the human race will survive the next thousand years unless we spread into space. I believe we will eventually establish self-sustaining colonies in space, and this may be the only way we survive."

9. WILL ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE OUTSMART US?
The development of full artificial general intelligence (AGI) could be the most transformative event in human history — or the last. Hawking is not opposed to AI in principle. He acknowledges it has already brought enormous benefits in medicine, science, and everyday life. But he worries about the "control problem": ensuring that a superintelligent AI's goals remain aligned with human values. "I believe that the rise of powerful AI will be either the best or the worst thing ever to happen to humanity. We do not yet know which." He calls for international cooperation on AI safety research and urges that the economic benefits of AI be shared widely, rather than accruing only to a small elite — otherwise AI could dramatically worsen inequality.

10. HOW DO WE SHAPE THE FUTURE?
Hawking ends with a call to action. He urges young people in particular to pursue science and to see themselves as participants in the great human enterprise of understanding and improving the world. He warns against the retreat of science — the denial of climate change, the defunding of research, the rise of anti-intellectualism. "We need to be outward-looking, to explore the cosmos with an open and curious mind." His final message: "Remember to look up at the stars and not down at your feet. Try to make sense of what you see and wonder about what makes the universe exist. Be curious. And however difficult life may seem, there is always something you can do and succeed at. It matters that you don't just give up. Unleash your imagination. Shape the future."

KEY QUOTES FROM THE BOOK

"We are the products of quantum processes ourselves. It is quantum mechanics that explains why atoms are stable, why chemistry exists, and why the sun shines."

"I was not a good student. I did not pay attention in class. I was always bored at school. But I was very interested in how things worked."

"Science is beautiful when it makes simple explanations of phenomena or connections between different observations."

"I have spent my whole life trying to understand the laws of the universe. We are very close to answering some very deep questions about how the universe began and whether it is the only one."

"In my experience, the people who make the greatest contribution to science are those who combine curiosity with imagination and rigorous thinking."

"If I had to choose a superpower, I'd choose to make fusion work. Because then we would have an inexhaustible source of clean energy and could stop burning fossil fuels."

"There is no unique picture of reality — a good model is one that agrees with the observations."

SIGNIFICANCE

Brief Answers to the Big Questions is the book in which Hawking speaks most directly to the big existential questions of our time. It is not primarily a book about physics, but about what physics means for how we live, what we value, and what we must do. Written as he was dying, it carries the weight of a final testament from a man who spent his life thinking about the deepest questions and who wanted to leave humanity with both his answers and his urgency.


Is there a God? Hawking concludes there is no God. He explains that before we understood science, it was natural to believe that God created the universe, but now science offers a more convincing explanation. Because there is a law such as gravity, the universe can and will create itself from nothing. He states that spontaneous creation is the reason there is something rather than nothing, why the universe exists, why we exist. It is not necessary to invoke God to light the blue touch paper and set the universe going.

How did it all begin? The universe started with the Big Bang, expanding from a state of infinite density and temperature. Time itself began at the Big Bang, meaning it makes no sense to ask what happened before it, as there was no "before".
