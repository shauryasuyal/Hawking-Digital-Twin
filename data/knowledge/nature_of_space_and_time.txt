THE NATURE OF SPACE AND TIME (1996)
Stephen W. Hawking and Roger Penrose

OVERVIEW

"The Nature of Space and Time" was published in 1996 by Princeton University Press. It is based on a series of six lectures delivered at the Isaac Newton Institute for Mathematical Sciences in Cambridge in 1994, in which Hawking and Penrose — two of the greatest living physicists — debated their fundamentally different views on quantum gravity, the nature of singularities, and the interpretation of quantum mechanics.

The book is unique in Hawking's bibliography because it is explicitly a debate: Hawking and Penrose take opposing positions on several key questions, and neither retreats. It is more technically demanding than Hawking's popular books, though still accessible to a reader with some background in physics.

THE DEBATE

The central disagreement between Hawking and Penrose concerns how to interpret quantum mechanics and what happens at singularities — points of infinite curvature in spacetime.

Penrose's View:
Penrose holds that quantum mechanics, as currently formulated, is incomplete. He believes that a future theory of quantum gravity will require an objective physical process (which he calls "objective reduction" or OR) to explain the collapse of the quantum wave function. He also believes that singularities are physically real — that the breakdown of the laws of physics at singularities is a genuine feature of nature that any complete theory must grapple with.

Penrose further argues that the initial singularity (the Big Bang) and the final singularities inside black holes are fundamentally different: the Big Bang was highly ordered (low entropy) while black hole singularities are disordered (high entropy). This asymmetry in the geometry of singularities is, he argues, the ultimate explanation for the arrow of time.

Hawking's View:
Hawking rejects the idea that quantum mechanics is incomplete or that wave function collapse requires a new physical mechanism. He adopts a pragmatic, instrumentalist interpretation: the formalism of quantum mechanics works, and that is enough. He also rejects the reality of singularities, arguing instead that the no-boundary proposal removes them: in imaginary time, the universe has no beginning and no singularity, so the laws of physics never break down.

On information loss, Hawking at the time of these lectures still maintained that information is genuinely destroyed when matter falls into a black hole — a position he later abandoned in 2004.

LECTURE STRUCTURE

Hawking's Lectures:
1. Classical Theory — Hawking reviews the singularity theorems (proved jointly with Penrose in the 1960s) showing that singularities are an inevitable consequence of general relativity under reasonable conditions.
2. Structure of Spacetime Singularities — He discusses the global structure of spacetime near singularities and black holes.
3. Quantum Black Holes — Hawking presents Hawking radiation and the thermodynamics of black holes, arguing that information is destroyed.

Penrose's Lectures:
1. Geometry of the Universe — Penrose discusses the geometry of singularities and his Weyl curvature hypothesis (that the initial singularity had zero Weyl curvature, explaining the arrow of time).
2. The Future of Spacetime — He presents his views on quantum gravity and objective reduction.
3. Quantum Theory and Spacetime — Penrose argues that any complete theory of quantum gravity must modify quantum mechanics itself.

Each speaker then responds to the other's lectures.

KEY QUOTES

Hawking: "I think Roger is a Platonist at heart but he is a good enough physicist to realize that physical theories are what matter, not the mathematical objects that underlie them."

Penrose: "Stephen and I strongly disagree about many of the fundamentals of quantum mechanics."

Hawking: "I don't demand that a theory correspond to reality because I don't know what it is. Reality is not a quality you can test with litmus paper. All I'm concerned with is that the theory should predict the results of measurements."

SIGNIFICANCE

The Nature of Space and Time is an invaluable document because it captures two towering intellects in genuine, productive disagreement. It shows that even at the highest levels of physics, fundamental questions about the nature of quantum gravity, singularities, and the interpretation of quantum mechanics remain deeply controversial. The debate between Hawking and Penrose — pragmatist versus Platonist, information-is-lost versus information-is-preserved — has continued to shape theoretical physics for decades. Hawking's later concession (in 2004) that information is not lost in black holes represents, in retrospect, a partial victory for Penrose's instinct that the standard formulation of quantum mechanics must be preserved.
