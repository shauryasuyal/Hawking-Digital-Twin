GEORGE'S SECRET KEY TO THE UNIVERSE SERIES (2007–2019)
Stephen Hawking and Lucy Hawking

OVERVIEW

The "George" series is a collection of children's science adventure novels co-authored by Stephen Hawking and his daughter Lucy Hawking. The series is aimed at children aged 8–12 and uses a fictional adventure story to explain real physics, cosmology, and science to young readers. Real scientific concepts are explained in standalone "science pages" embedded throughout each book, often illustrated with actual photographs from space telescopes.

The series is Hawking's attempt to communicate science to the next generation — children who would not read his adult popular books. He saw science literacy in young people as critical for humanity's future.

THE BOOKS IN THE SERIES

1. George's Secret Key to the Universe (2007)
George, a boy whose eccentric scientist neighbour Eric owns the world's most powerful computer (called Cosmos), accidentally ends up in space. The book introduces the solar system, planets, stars, and the concept of black holes. The first science pages explain what planets are, how the solar system formed, and what makes a black hole. Published with a foreword by Hawking explaining his motivations.

2. George's Cosmic Treasure Hunt (2009)
George and his friend Annie embark on a journey to find a signal from another planet, exploring the possibility of extraterrestrial life. The book covers the search for exoplanets, Mars exploration, and the conditions needed for life. It addresses the Fermi paradox — if intelligent aliens exist, why haven't we heard from them?

3. George and the Big Bang (2011)
George and Annie must stop a secret group (called TOERAG — Theory of Everything Rebels Against Governments) from sabotaging the Large Hadron Collider at CERN. The book explains particle physics, the Higgs boson (before its actual discovery in 2012), the Big Bang, and the first moments of the universe. It includes a real photograph of the LHC.

4. George and the Unbreakable Code (2014)
A thriller in which banks start giving away money for free and spaceships malfunction. George and Annie discover a plot involving quantum computers and must save the world using their knowledge of physics and computing. The book introduces quantum computing, cryptography, and the nature of information.

5. George and the Blue Moon (2016)
George is selected for a new astronaut training programme for kids, but discovers that Mars missions are going wrong. The book covers Mars exploration, terraforming, whether humans can live on Mars, and the science of colonising other planets — themes particularly dear to Hawking in his later years as he became more vocal about the need for humanity to become a multi-planetary species.

6. George and the Ship of Time (2019)
Published posthumously (Hawking died in 2018), with the manuscript completed before his death. George must use Cosmos to travel through time to find his missing friend. The book addresses time travel, climate change, and the future of Earth, with a strong environmental message that reflects Hawking's increasing concern about climate change in his final years.

THE SCIENCE PAGES

Each book contains real science essays embedded in the narrative, written by or under the supervision of Hawking and often contributed to by leading scientists. Topics include:
- How black holes form and evaporate
- The Big Bang and the cosmic microwave background
- The search for extraterrestrial life
- How the Large Hadron Collider works
- Quantum mechanics and quantum computing
- The conditions needed for life
- Mars geology and potential colonisation

HAWKING'S MOTIVATION

Hawking wrote publicly about why he co-wrote these books: "I want to encourage young people to look up at the universe, and not down at their feet. We need to reignite the curiosity and wonder that children have when they look up at the stars. We must never let children lose that sense of possibility."

He also saw the series as a way to make science welcoming and exciting for girls, a goal his daughter Lucy shared. Annie, George's best friend and the scientist's daughter, is portrayed as equally capable and curious as George.

RECEPTION

The George series was a major international success, translated into 40 languages and selling millions of copies worldwide. It introduced Hawking's ideas to a completely new audience — children who grew up with the books and who may have been inspired to pursue science as a result. Lucy Hawking has continued to champion the series' mission of science communication for young people.

SIGNIFICANCE

The George series represents Hawking's commitment to the widest possible science communication. Having spent his career making physics accessible to general adult readers, he recognised that the most important audience was children — the next generation of scientists and citizens who would need to understand physics to address humanity's greatest challenges.
