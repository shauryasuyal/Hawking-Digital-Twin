A BRIEFER HISTORY OF TIME (2005)
Stephen W. Hawking and Leonard Mlodinow

OVERVIEW

"A Briefer History of Time" was published in 2005 by Bantam Books, co-authored by Stephen Hawking and physicist and science writer Leonard Mlodinow. It is a substantially revised and updated version of "A Brief History of Time" (1988), written to be more accessible to readers who found the original challenging, and to incorporate the major scientific developments of the intervening seventeen years — particularly the rise of string theory and M-theory.

Despite its title, A Briefer History of Time is not simply a condensed version of the original. Mlodinow's co-authorship brought a new voice and clarity, and the book adds significant new material on string theory, M-theory, and the nature of physical laws that was not present in the original. Several chapters were entirely rewritten.

WHY IT WAS WRITTEN

Hawking acknowledged that despite the extraordinary success of A Brief History of Time, many readers found it difficult. The famous joke — "the most popular book never finished" — reflected a real problem. The original had been written in a rush, with some sections that were genuinely demanding. Hawking also wanted to respond to the remarkable progress in theoretical physics since 1988, particularly the "second superstring revolution" of the mid-1990s and the development of M-theory.

KEY CHANGES AND ADDITIONS

String Theory and M-Theory
The most significant addition to A Briefer History of Time is an extended treatment of string theory and M-theory. The book explains how string theory replaces point particles with tiny vibrating strings whose different modes of vibration correspond to different particles. Hawking explains the five consistent versions of string theory that emerged in the 1980s, and how in the 1990s these were shown to be five different limits of a single eleven-dimensional theory called M-theory.

The chapter on M-theory explains how the theory allows for approximately 10^500 different possible universes, each with different physical laws and constants — the "landscape" of string theory — and how this makes the anthropic principle relevant: we live in a universe with constants compatible with our existence because we could not exist in the others.

Clearer Exposition
Mlodinow's contribution is most visible in the clarity of the prose. Many passages that readers found opaque in the original were substantially rewritten. The treatment of quantum mechanics, in particular, is more gradual and accessible.

Updated Cosmology
The book incorporates observational discoveries made since 1988, including:
- The 1998 discovery that the expansion of the universe is accelerating (dark energy)
- Improved measurements of the cosmic microwave background
- Evidence for the inflationary model of the early universe

STRUCTURE

Part 1: Our Picture of the Universe — History of cosmology from Aristotle to Einstein.
Part 2: Space and Time — Special and general relativity, the nature of spacetime.
Part 3: The Expanding Universe — The Big Bang and observational evidence.
Part 4: Elementary Particles and the Forces of Nature — Particles, forces, and the quest for unification.
Part 5: Quantum Theory — Wave-particle duality, the uncertainty principle, quantum mechanics.
Part 6: Black Holes — Formation, structure, Hawking radiation, information paradox.
Part 7: The Origin and Fate of the Universe — The no-boundary proposal, inflation, the fate of the universe.
Part 8: The Unification of Physics — String theory, M-theory, the multiverse.
Part 9: Conclusion — The quest for a Theory of Everything.

KEY QUOTES

"Even if there is only one possible unified theory, it is just a set of rules and equations. What is it that breathes fire into the equations and makes a universe for them to describe?"

"The whole history of science has been the gradual realization that events do not happen in an arbitrary manner, but that they reflect a certain underlying order, which may or may not be divinely inspired."

"In quantum mechanics, particles don't have single definite histories. Every possible history must be summed up."

RECEPTION

A Briefer History of Time was well received, though it did not replicate the extraordinary phenomenon of the original. Critics praised its improved clarity while noting that some of the original's distinctive voice had been softened. It remains an excellent introduction to Hawking's ideas for readers who found A Brief History of Time too challenging.

SIGNIFICANCE

The book represents Hawking's attempt to ensure his ideas remained accessible as physics evolved. Its treatment of string theory and M-theory made these concepts available to a general audience for the first time in a truly accessible form, before The Grand Design (2010) addressed them in even greater depth.
