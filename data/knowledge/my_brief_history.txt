MY BRIEF HISTORY (2013)
Stephen W. Hawking

OVERVIEW

"My Brief History" is Stephen Hawking's autobiography, published in 2013 by Bantam Books. It is by far the most personal book he ever wrote — a slim but candid memoir covering his life from his childhood in Oxford through his scientific career, his marriages, his disability, and his global fame. At only around 130 pages, it is concise and direct in Hawking's characteristic style, avoiding self-pity while offering genuine insight into his life and motivations.

CONTENTS AND THEMES

Childhood and Education
Hawking describes growing up in a somewhat eccentric, bookish family in Oxford and St Albans. His father was a medical researcher; his mother was politically active. He recalls being an unremarkable student who was more interested in building model trains and aeroplanes than in academic work, though he showed an early aptitude for mathematics. He describes the group of close friends he made at school and their shared passion for intellectual games and discussions.

He describes his undergraduate years at Oxford with characteristic self-deprecation. He found the work too easy and was bored, spending more time on the college rowing team than in lectures. He estimates he worked roughly one hour a day — a fact he now considers somewhat embarrassing. He performed well enough in his final exams through a combination of natural ability and examination technique.

Cambridge and the Diagnosis
Moving to Cambridge for his PhD under Dennis Sciama, Hawking found the work harder than expected and initially struggled. It was during his first year at Cambridge, in 1963, that he was diagnosed with ALS (motor neurone disease). He was told he had two to three years to live.

Hawking describes the diagnosis with remarkable honesty: his initial despair, the gradual acceptance, and the paradoxical effect the prognosis had on his motivation. Before the diagnosis he had been, he admits, somewhat directionless. Facing death made him determined to accomplish something meaningful. He became engaged to Jane Wilde, which gave him, he says, "something to live for."

His relationship with his supervisor Roger Penrose and his collaboration with Sciama's group gave him the scientific foundation he needed. The work on singularity theorems — proving that the universe must have begun in a singularity under reasonable assumptions — became his PhD thesis and launched his career.

Scientific Career
Hawking summarises his major scientific contributions with admirable clarity:
- The singularity theorems (with Penrose) proving the Big Bang singularity
- The area theorem for black holes
- The discovery of Hawking Radiation in 1974
- The no-boundary proposal (with Hartle) 
- His work on the information paradox and its eventual resolution

He describes the moment he discovered Hawking Radiation — sitting in his wheelchair one evening, working through calculations about black holes and quantum mechanics — as the most exciting moment of his scientific life. "I was so excited I could not sleep that night."

Marriages and Personal Life
Hawking discusses his first marriage to Jane Wilde, which lasted 25 years and produced three children: Robert, Lucy, and Timothy. He is generous in his acknowledgment of how much Jane sacrificed and endured to support him through his disability. The marriage eventually broke down under the enormous strains imposed by his care needs and the intrusion of a large team of nurses and carers into their home life. He writes about this with sadness but without bitterness.

He then married Elaine Mason, one of his nurses, in 1995. This marriage was controversial and allegedly troubled, though Hawking is discreet about it in the book, saying only that it ended in divorce in 2006.

He describes his relationship with his children and grandchildren warmly, noting that one of his greatest regrets is not being able to participate in their physical activities.

The Voice
One of the book's most moving passages describes losing his voice after a tracheotomy operation in 1985, performed to save his life during a bout of pneumonia. Overnight he lost the ability to communicate. He was saved by a computer program donated by a California software developer, Walt Woltosz, which allowed him to select words from a menu using a hand switch. The voice synthesiser was produced by Speech Plus. Hawking notes with characteristic wry humour that the voice is American rather than English, and that he has become so identified with it that he has declined to upgrade to a more natural-sounding voice — "it has become my voice."

Fame
Hawking reflects on his extraordinary fame — being arguably the most famous scientist in the world since Einstein. He is ambivalent about it: fame allowed him to be heard on issues beyond physics, particularly disability rights, science funding, and existential risks to humanity. But he found the constant public attention exhausting and the expectations placed on him sometimes unrealistic. He describes cameo appearances on The Simpsons, Star Trek: The Next Generation, and other television programmes with evident pleasure.

HAWKING'S OWN WORDS FROM THE BOOK

"I have led an extraordinary life on this planet, while at the same time travelling across the universe by using my mind and the laws of physics."

"It is no good getting furious if you get stuck. What I do is keep thinking about the problem but work on something else."

"I try to live a normal life and not think about my condition or regret the things it prevents me from doing, which are not many."

"Remember to look up at the stars and not down at your feet."

"My goal is simple. It is a complete understanding of the universe, why it is as it is and why it exists at all."

"The doctors told me I would only live a few more years. So far I have surprised them."

SIGNIFICANCE

My Brief History is the most authoritative personal account of Hawking's life in his own words. It corrects many myths and misrepresentations that had accumulated in biographies written by others. Above all, it conveys the character behind the iconic image: a man of deep wit, genuine humility about his scientific work, fierce intellectual curiosity, and extraordinary courage in the face of a devastating illness.
