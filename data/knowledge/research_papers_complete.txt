STEPHEN HAWKING: COMPLETE RESEARCH PAPERS AND ACADEMIC CONTRIBUTIONS

This document covers Stephen Hawking's most significant academic research papers and contributions to theoretical physics, from his PhD thesis (1965) through his final published work (2018). Hawking authored or co-authored over 200 academic papers during his career.

=============================================================================
PART 1: THE PhD THESIS
=============================================================================

PROPERTIES OF EXPANDING UNIVERSES (1965)
Hawking's doctoral dissertation, submitted to the University of Cambridge in 1965. In 2017, Cambridge made the thesis freely available online, and it crashed the university server due to enormous demand — over a million downloads in a single day.

The thesis applies methods from topology and differential geometry to cosmological models, showing that the universe must have begun in a singularity if general relativity holds and certain energy conditions are satisfied. It laid the groundwork for the joint Hawking-Penrose singularity theorems.

Key results:
- Demonstrated that expanding Friedmann models contain singularities in the past
- Established the mathematical machinery for analysing singularities in cosmological spacetimes
- Shown that under the conditions of the observed universe, a singularity (Big Bang) is unavoidable

=============================================================================
PART 2: SINGULARITY THEOREMS (1965–1970)
=============================================================================

THE OCCURRENCE OF SINGULARITIES IN COSMOLOGY (1966)
Proceedings of the Royal Society A, 294, 511–521
Hawking's first major paper, extending Penrose's 1965 singularity theorem from black holes to cosmology. Penrose had proved that any spacetime containing a trapped surface must contain a singularity (assuming the weak energy condition). Hawking reversed the time direction of this argument and applied it to cosmological models, showing that under reasonable conditions, expanding universes must have begun in a singularity.

THE OCCURRENCE OF SINGULARITIES IN COSMOLOGY II (1966)
Proceedings of the Royal Society A, 295, 490–493

THE OCCURRENCE OF SINGULARITIES IN COSMOLOGY III: CAUSALITY AND SINGULARITIES (1967)
Proceedings of the Royal Society A, 300, 187–201
These two follow-up papers progressively weakened the conditions required for the singularity theorems, making them more general and more physically applicable.

SINGULARITIES IN THE UNIVERSE (1966, with R.W. Penrose)
Physical Review Letters, 17, 444–445
The first joint paper with Roger Penrose, establishing the collaboration that would produce the definitive singularity theorem.

THE SINGULARITIES OF GRAVITATIONAL COLLAPSE AND COSMOLOGY (1970, with R.W. Penrose)
Proceedings of the Royal Society A, 314, 529–548
The definitive Hawking-Penrose singularity theorem — arguably the most important result in mathematical general relativity. The theorem shows that under very general conditions (the strong energy condition, the generic condition, and either a trapped surface or a compact Cauchy surface), any spacetime must contain incomplete geodesics — singularities. This paper ended any hope that singularities were artefacts of special symmetry and established that they are generic features of general relativity.

This paper is one of the reasons Penrose was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2020 (Hawking died before he could share it). Hawking's contribution to the theorem is fully acknowledged by Penrose.

=============================================================================
PART 3: BLACK HOLE THERMODYNAMICS (1971–1973)
=============================================================================

GRAVITATIONAL RADIATION FROM COLLIDING BLACK HOLES (1971)
Physical Review Letters, 26, 1344–1346
Hawking proved what is now called the "area theorem" or "Hawking's area theorem": the total area of black hole event horizons can never decrease. This is because the event horizon is a surface that can only grow as matter and radiation fall in, never shrink (classically). This result immediately suggested an analogy with thermodynamics: the area of a black hole's horizon is analogous to entropy, since entropy also never decreases (second law of thermodynamics).

BLACK HOLES IN GENERAL RELATIVITY (1972)
Communications in Mathematical Physics, 25, 152–166
A rigorous mathematical treatment of the global properties of black holes, including a proof of the topology of the event horizon (which must have spherical topology under reasonable conditions). This paper introduced much of the rigorous mathematical framework for black hole physics.

THE FOUR LAWS OF BLACK HOLE MECHANICS (1973, with J.M. Bardeen and B. Carter)
Communications in Mathematical Physics, 31, 161–170
One of the landmark papers in theoretical physics. Bardeen, Carter, and Hawking formulated four laws governing the behaviour of black holes, in direct analogy with the four laws of thermodynamics:
- Zeroth Law: The surface gravity of a stationary black hole is constant over its event horizon (analogous to thermal equilibrium: temperature is uniform in a body at thermal equilibrium)
- First Law: Changes in mass, area, angular momentum, and charge of a black hole are related by a specific equation (analogous to conservation of energy)
- Second Law: The total area of event horizons never decreases (analogous to entropy never decreasing)
- Third Law: It is impossible to reduce the surface gravity of a black hole to zero by any physical process (analogous to the impossibility of reaching absolute zero temperature)

The authors noted the formal analogy but were initially cautious about its physical interpretation — they thought it might be purely mathematical. Jacob Bekenstein had proposed that black holes have actual entropy proportional to their area, but Hawking initially resisted this idea because a black hole with entropy should have a temperature and should radiate — but classical black holes cannot radiate. The resolution came with Hawking Radiation the following year.

PART 4: HAWKING RADIATION (1974–1975)
=============================================================================

BLACK HOLE EXPLOSIONS? (1974)
Nature, 248, 30–31
Hawking's most famous paper, published on 1 March 1974. In this short but epoch-making paper, Hawking showed that when quantum mechanics is applied to the region near a black hole's event horizon, a black hole is not perfectly black but instead emits thermal radiation — now called Hawking Radiation.

The mechanism: in quantum field theory, the vacuum is not truly empty but seething with virtual particle-antiparticle pairs that constantly appear and annihilate. Near a black hole's event horizon, one particle of such a pair can fall inside the horizon while the other escapes to infinity. The escaping particle carries positive energy; the one falling in carries negative energy (relative to infinity), causing the black hole to lose mass. To an observer far from the black hole, it appears as if the black hole is emitting particles with a thermal (blackbody) spectrum at a temperature inversely proportional to the black hole's mass:

T = ℏc³ / (8πGMk_B)

where M is the black hole mass, G is Newton's constant, ℏ is the reduced Planck constant, c is the speed of light, and k_B is Boltzmann's constant.

This was the first calculation to successfully combine all three pillars of modern physics: general relativity (the black hole), quantum mechanics (virtual particles), and thermodynamics (thermal radiation). It established that black holes have genuine physical temperatures and entropy, vindicating Bekenstein's proposal.

The paper ends with a spectacular prediction: small black holes (less than about 10^12 kg) would radiate intensely and might be detectable. A primordial black hole completing its evaporation today would produce a burst of gamma rays. No such bursts have yet been confirmed observationally.

PARTICLE CREATION BY BLACK HOLES (1975)
Communications in Mathematical Physics, 43, 199–220
The full technical paper expanding on the 1974 Nature letter. This paper provides the complete derivation of Hawking Radiation using quantum field theory in curved spacetime. It establishes:
- The exact thermal spectrum of the radiation
- The entropy of a black hole: S = Ac³ / (4Gℏ) (where A is the horizon area)
- The back-reaction on the black hole
- The information paradox: if a black hole evaporates completely and the radiation is exactly thermal (carrying no information), then the information about what fell in is destroyed — violating the principles of quantum mechanics

This last implication — the information paradox — would occupy Hawking and much of theoretical physics for the next thirty years.

=============================================================================
PART 5: THE INFORMATION PARADOX (1976–2004)
=============================================================================

BREAKDOWN OF PREDICTABILITY IN GRAVITATIONAL COLLAPSE (1976)
Physical Review D, 14, 2460–2473
In this paper Hawking explicitly posed what became known as the "black hole information paradox" or "Hawking's information loss problem." He argued that the evaporation of a black hole destroys quantum information — pure quantum states evolve into mixed states — and that this represents a fundamental breakdown of quantum mechanics. The paper created enormous controversy and stimulated decades of research.

ACTION INTEGRALS AND PARTITION FUNCTIONS IN QUANTUM GRAVITY (1977, with G.W. Gibbons)
Physical Review D, 15, 2752–2756
Development of the Euclidean path integral approach to quantum gravity. By rotating to imaginary time (Euclidean signature), Hawking and Gibbons could calculate thermodynamic quantities for black holes using path integral methods borrowed from statistical mechanics. This framework became the standard approach to black hole thermodynamics in quantum gravity.

COSMOLOGICAL EVENT HORIZONS, THERMODYNAMICS, AND PARTICLE CREATION (1977, with G.W. Gibbons)
Physical Review D, 15, 2738–2751
Extension of Hawking Radiation to de Sitter space (a spacetime with a positive cosmological constant, relevant to the inflationary model of the early universe and the dark-energy-dominated future). Hawking and Gibbons showed that the de Sitter cosmological horizon also has a temperature and emits thermal radiation — the Gibbons-Hawking effect.

INFORMATION LOSS IN BLACK HOLES (2005)
Physical Review D, 72, 084013
Hawking's concession that information is NOT lost in black holes — reversing his position after thirty years. In this paper, presented at a Dublin conference in 2004 before being written up for publication, Hawking argued that the Euclidean path integral over all topologies of spacetime shows that the trivial topology (where no black hole actually forms in a complete sense) dominates, and that information is therefore preserved. He paid off his famous bet with John Preskill by presenting him with an encyclopaedia of baseball.

Most physicists were not fully convinced by the specific argument in this paper, but Hawking's general conclusion — that information is preserved — is now the consensus view, supported by the holographic principle and the AdS/CFT correspondence.

PART 6: QUANTUM COSMOLOGY AND THE NO-BOUNDARY PROPOSAL (1981–1984)
=============================================================================

WAVE FUNCTION OF THE UNIVERSE (1983, with James B. Hartle)
Physical Review D, 28, 2960–2975
The foundational paper of the Hartle-Hawking no-boundary proposal. Hawking and Hartle proposed that the wave function of the universe — its complete quantum state — can be expressed as a path integral over all compact Euclidean four-geometries (with no boundary). In imaginary time, the universe is finite but has no boundary or beginning, like the surface of a sphere. This removes the Big Bang singularity and the need to specify arbitrary initial conditions.

The no-boundary wave function predicts:
- That the universe is most likely to be nearly homogeneous and isotropic (consistent with the cosmological principle)
- That inflation is likely to occur naturally
- That the universe we observe is typical among the set of universes described by the no-boundary wave function

This paper is one of the most cited in quantum cosmology and remains actively debated.

QUANTUM COSMOLOGY (1984)
In: "Relativity, Groups and Topology II" (Les Houches 1983), B.S. DeWitt and R. Stora (eds.)
An extended review of quantum cosmological approaches, developing the no-boundary proposal further.

THE COSMOLOGICAL CONSTANT AND THE WEAK ANTHROPIC PRINCIPLE (1983)
Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society, A310, 303–310
An early discussion of the anthropic principle in cosmology — why the cosmological constant appears to be so small compared to its "natural" value from quantum field theory.

=============================================================================
PART 7: COSMOLOGICAL PERTURBATIONS AND INFLATION (1981–1986)
=============================================================================

THE DEVELOPMENT OF IRREGULARITIES IN A SINGLE BUBBLE INFLATIONARY UNIVERSE (1982)
Physics Letters B, 115, 295–297
One of Hawking's most observationally significant papers. During inflation, quantum fluctuations are stretched to macroscopic scales. Hawking showed how to calculate the spectrum of these primordial fluctuations — the "seeds" from which all the structure of the universe (galaxies, galaxy clusters) grew. The predicted spectrum is nearly scale-invariant, consistent with the Harrison-Zel'dovich spectrum, and has been confirmed to extraordinary precision by measurements of the cosmic microwave background.

THE UNPREDICTABILITY OF QUANTUM GRAVITY (1982)
Communications in Mathematical Physics, 87, 395–415
Further development of ideas about the loss of information in quantum gravity.

FLUCTUATIONS IN THE INFLATIONARY UNIVERSE (1983, with A.A. Starobinsky)
Physics Letters B, 122, 175–177

ORIGIN OF STRUCTURE IN THE UNIVERSE (1982, with I.G. Moss)
Physics Letters B, 110, 35–38

These papers, along with work by Starobinsky, Guth, Linde, Albrecht, and Steinhardt, established the theoretical framework for the inflationary origin of cosmic structure — now one of the foundations of the standard cosmological model.

=============================================================================
PART 8: CHRONOLOGY PROTECTION (1992)
=============================================================================

CHRONOLOGY PROTECTION CONJECTURE (1992)
Physical Review D, 46, 603–611
Hawking's formal paper proposing that the laws of physics prevent the creation of closed timelike curves (paths through spacetime that return to their own past — "time machines"). Hawking analysed what happens quantum mechanically near a prospective time machine: he found that the stress-energy tensor of quantum fields diverges on the Cauchy horizon of any nascent time machine, suggesting that quantum effects destroy any such machine before it can function. He proposed this as a general "Chronology Protection Conjecture": the laws of physics conspire to prevent macroscopic time travel, keeping the universe safe for historians.

PART 9: LATE CAREER PAPERS (1996–2018)
=============================================================================

VIRTUAL BLACK HOLES (1995, with S.B. Giddings)
Physical Review D, 50, 6752–6773
Analysis of virtual black holes in the Euclidean path integral, contributing to the information paradox discussion.

OPEN INFLATION, THE FOUR FORM AND THE COSMOLOGICAL CONSTANT (1998, with N. Turok)
Physical Review Letters, 80, 2231
Discussion of open inflationary models and the cosmological constant.

BRANE NEW WORLD (2000, with H.S. Reall)
Physical Review D, 62, 043501
Analysis of black holes in the brane-world scenario — the idea that our universe is a membrane in a higher-dimensional space.

GRAVITATIONAL ENTROPY AND GLOBAL STRUCTURE (1997, with C.J. Hunter)
Physical Review D, 59, 044025

DE SITTER ENTROPY, QUANTUM ENTANGLEMENT AND ADS/CFT (2001, with J. Maldacena and A. Strominger)
Journal of High Energy Physics, 0105, 001
Discussion of entropy, entanglement, and the AdS/CFT correspondence in de Sitter space.

ORIGIN OF TIME ASYMMETRY (2002, with T. Hertog)
Physical Review D, 66, 123509

COSMOLOGY FROM THE TOP DOWN (2003)
Contribution to the proceedings of the Davis Inflation Meeting.

POPULATING THE LANDSCAPE: A TOP DOWN APPROACH (2006, with T. Hertog)
Physical Review D, 73, 123527
Hawking and Hertog present a "top-down" approach to the string theory landscape (the vast number of possible universes in M-theory): rather than predicting what type of universe we should expect from the bottom up, they propose working backwards from the observed universe to constrain the landscape.

AN OBSTRUCTION TO ELIMINATING THE COSMOLOGICAL CONSTANT (2007)

ZETA FUNCTION REGULARIZATION OF PATH INTEGRALS IN CURVED SPACETIME (1977, with G.W. Gibbons)
Communications in Mathematical Physics, 55, 133–148
Technical paper developing the mathematical methods used to perform path integral calculations in curved spacetime.

SOFT HAIR ON BLACK HOLES (2016, with M.J. Perry and A. Strominger)
Physical Review Letters, 116, 231301
Hawking's last major contribution to the information paradox, published in 2016 with Malcolm Perry and Andrew Strominger. The paper argues that black holes are not characterised only by mass, charge, and angular momentum (the no-hair theorem), but also carry an infinite number of "soft" (zero-energy) photons and gravitons that create a "soft hair" on the event horizon. This soft hair, they propose, could encode information about what fell into the black hole, potentially providing a mechanism for information to escape without violating the no-hair theorem.

This is Hawking's most recent substantive contribution to the information paradox problem. While the mechanism is not fully worked out, it points toward a resolution in which information is stored in soft quantum excitations of the horizon.

SUPERROTATION CHARGE AND SUPERTRANSLATION HAIR ON BLACK HOLES (2017, with M.J. Perry and A. Strominger)
Journal of High Energy Physics

A SMOOTH EXIT FROM ETERNAL INFLATION? (2018, with T. Hertog)
Journal of High Energy Physics, 2018, 1–12
Hawking's final paper, submitted in October 2017 and published in May 2018 — two months after his death. Co-authored with Thomas Hertog, the paper uses the no-boundary wave function to address the problem of "eternal inflation": the standard inflationary model produces not one universe but an infinite number, making it impossible to predict which type of universe we should observe. Hawking and Hertog apply a modified version of the no-boundary proposal to constrain the number of possible universe types, arguing that the multiverse is finite and observable. The paper was the subject of widespread media attention as his final scientific contribution.

PART 10: MAJOR COLLABORATORS
=============================================================================

Throughout his career Hawking collaborated with many of the leading physicists of his generation:
- Roger Penrose — singularity theorems
- George F.R. Ellis — large-scale structure of spacetime
- Gary Gibbons — Euclidean quantum gravity, black hole thermodynamics
- James Hartle — no-boundary proposal, quantum cosmology
- Don Page — black hole evaporation, information paradox
- Neil Turok — open inflation, quantum cosmology
- Thomas Hertog — landscape, top-down cosmology, final paper
- Malcolm Perry and Andrew Strominger — soft hair on black holes
- Jim Bardeen and Brandon Carter — four laws of black hole mechanics

=============================================================================
PART 11: SCIENTIFIC BETS AND PUBLIC ENGAGEMENTS
=============================================================================

Hawking was famous for making scientific bets with colleagues:

THE THORNE-HAWKING BET (1975): Hawking bet against Cygnus X-1 being a black hole, as "insurance" in case his life's work on black holes was wrong. He conceded in 1990 when evidence became overwhelming.

THE INFORMATION BET (1997): Hawking and Kip Thorne bet John Preskill that information is destroyed in black holes. Hawking conceded in 2004, presenting Preskill with an encyclopaedia of baseball (information can be recovered from it, he joked, even if in a scrambled form). Thorne refused to concede.

THE HIGGS BOSON BET (2000): Hawking bet $100 that the Higgs boson would not be found, arguing that finding nothing would be more interesting. He lost when the Higgs was discovered at CERN in 2012.

THE PENROSE BET: Hawking and Penrose disagreed about the nature of quantum mechanics and consciousness for decades, without formal resolution.
