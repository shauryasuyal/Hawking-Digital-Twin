THE LARGE, THE SMALL AND THE HUMAN MIND (1997)
Stephen W. Hawking
With responses by Roger Penrose, Abner Shimony, and Nancy Cartwright
Cambridge University Press

OVERVIEW

"The Large, the Small and the Human Mind" was published in 1997 by Cambridge University Press. It is based on the 1995 Tanner Lectures on Human Values delivered by Hawking at Cambridge. The book contains Hawking's lectures followed by responses from three distinguished thinkers — Roger Penrose (mathematical physicist), Abner Shimony (philosopher of physics), and Nancy Cartwright (philosopher of science) — and then Hawking's replies.

The book addresses three interlocking questions:
1. How does general relativity describe the universe on the largest scales? (The Large)
2. How does quantum mechanics describe the universe on the smallest scales? (The Small)
3. What is the relationship between these physical theories and the human mind? (The Human Mind)

HAWKING'S LECTURES

Lecture 1 — The Universe on the Large Scale
Hawking reviews general relativity and its consequences for cosmology: the expanding universe, the Big Bang, black holes, and the singularity theorems. He presents his no-boundary proposal with James Hartle: the universe has no beginning in imaginary time, removing the singularity and the need to specify initial conditions. He argues that this is the most natural and elegant description of the origin of the universe.

Lecture 2 — The Universe on the Small Scale
Hawking explains quantum mechanics — the uncertainty principle, wave-particle duality, quantum fields, and the sum-over-histories approach. He discusses Hawking radiation as a result of combining general relativity and quantum mechanics near a black hole's event horizon. He argues that information is lost in black holes (a position he later reversed in 2004).

Lecture 3 — The Origin of Structure
This lecture bridges the large and small scales. Hawking explains how quantum fluctuations in the very early universe — tiny ripples in the quantum fields — were stretched to macroscopic scales during cosmological inflation, seeding the large-scale structure we observe today: galaxies, galaxy clusters, and the cosmic web. He also introduces M-theory as the leading candidate for a unified theory.

Lecture 4 — The Human Mind
Hawking addresses the relationship between physics and consciousness. He is sceptical of claims that quantum mechanics has special implications for human consciousness. He argues that the mind is a physical system governed by physical laws, and that there is no reason in principle why a sufficiently complex computer or neural network could not replicate human cognitive functions. He discusses free will, determinism, and whether the human mind can ever understand itself.

THE RESPONSES AND DEBATE

Roger Penrose:
Penrose challenges Hawking's pragmatic approach to quantum mechanics, arguing that the theory must be modified at the level of quantum gravity to properly describe the collapse of the wave function. He presents his Orchestrated Objective Reduction (Orch-OR) model of consciousness, in which quantum processes in microtubules within neurons play a fundamental role in generating conscious experience. Hawking is unconvinced.

Abner Shimony:
Shimony focuses on the foundations of quantum mechanics, agreeing with Penrose that the measurement problem (what causes wave function collapse) is a genuine unsolved problem. He discusses the Bell inequalities and the experimental confirmation that quantum mechanics is genuinely non-local — a feature with profound philosophical implications.

Nancy Cartwright:
Cartwright, a philosopher of science, challenges both Hawking and Penrose from a different direction. She questions whether fundamental physical theories like quantum mechanics and general relativity really do describe "the world as it is" or whether they are better understood as useful models with limited domains of applicability. She is sceptical of the dream of a single unified Theory of Everything.

Hawking's Replies:
Hawking responds with characteristic directness. On consciousness: he remains convinced that it is a physical process and that Penrose's Orch-OR model is speculation without sufficient evidence. On the Theory of Everything: he maintains his optimism that physics is close to a complete theory. On the interpretation of quantum mechanics: he takes a pragmatist position — the theory works, and debates about its "true" meaning are philosophical rather than scientific.

KEY QUOTES

Hawking: "I take the positivist viewpoint that a physical theory is just a mathematical model and that it is meaningless to ask whether it corresponds to reality."

Hawking on consciousness: "I think the brain is a computer that will stop working when its components fail. The mind is a program that runs on that computer."

Penrose (response): "Hawking and I agree about the physics but disagree about what the physics means."

Hawking: "Some people would claim that things like love, joy and beauty belong to a different category from science and can never be explained by it. I think they make this claim because they feel that science is too cold and rational to explain the warmth and richness of human experience."

SIGNIFICANCE

The Large, the Small and the Human Mind is notable for engaging directly with the philosophical implications of physics — consciousness, free will, the nature of scientific knowledge — in a way that Hawking rarely did elsewhere. The multi-author format creates a genuine intellectual debate that is more rigorous than many popular science discussions. It also provides the clearest account Hawking ever gave of his pragmatist, instrumentalist philosophy of science: theories are tools for prediction, not pictures of reality.
