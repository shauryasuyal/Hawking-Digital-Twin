THE THEORY OF EVERYTHING: THE ORIGIN AND FATE OF THE UNIVERSE (2002)
Stephen W. Hawking

OVERVIEW

"The Theory of Everything: The Origin and Fate of the Universe" is a book by Stephen Hawking, published in 2002 by New Millennium Audio. It is based on a series of seven lectures Hawking delivered at Cambridge University, covering the history of ideas about the universe — from Aristotle and Newton through Einstein and modern cosmology — and culminating in the search for a single, unified theory that explains all of physical reality.

The book is sometimes confused with the 2014 biographical film of the same name about Hawking's life, starring Eddie Redmayne. The film is based on the memoir "Travelling to Infinity: My Life with Stephen" written by his first wife, Jane Wilde Hawking, not on this book directly. Hawking acknowledged the film warmly, saying Eddie Redmayne's portrayal made him feel he was watching himself.

WHY HAWKING WROTE IT

Hawking's central scientific ambition throughout his career was to find a "Theory of Everything" — a single, complete framework of physics that would reconcile general relativity (which governs the very large: stars, galaxies, the universe) with quantum mechanics (which governs the very small: particles, atoms). These two theories are individually extremely successful, but they are fundamentally incompatible with each other. A true Theory of Everything would dissolve that contradiction.

The lectures in this book represent Hawking's clearest public statement of that quest — the story of how we came to understand the universe and where that understanding was headed.

THE SEVEN LECTURES

Lecture 1 — Ideas About the Universe
Hawking traces the history of cosmological thought from Aristotle (who believed in a static, eternal universe with Earth at the center) through the Copernican revolution (Earth orbiting the Sun), Galileo's observations, and Newton's laws of gravity. He shows how each generation's "theory of everything" was eventually overturned by new observations. He introduces the concept that a truly correct theory must be internally consistent, must make testable predictions, and must not require special initial conditions selected by hand.

Lecture 2 — The Expanding Universe
Hawking explains Hubble's 1929 discovery that galaxies are moving away from us, and that the further away a galaxy is, the faster it recedes. This implies the universe is expanding. Running the expansion backward in time, all the matter and energy in the universe converges on a single point of infinite density — the Big Bang. Hawking discusses the cosmic microwave background radiation (discovered in 1965) as direct evidence of this hot, dense early state.

Lecture 3 — Black Holes
A black hole forms when a sufficiently massive star exhausts its nuclear fuel and collapses under its own gravity. The resulting gravitational field is so strong that nothing — not even light — can escape beyond a boundary called the event horizon. Hawking presents the no-hair theorem: no matter what kind of matter fell into a black hole, the resulting black hole is completely described by just three quantities: mass, electric charge, and angular momentum (spin). All other information is lost.

Lecture 4 — Black Holes Ain't So Black
This is the lecture where Hawking introduces his most famous scientific discovery — Hawking Radiation. Classical physics said black holes could only grow. But when Hawking applied quantum mechanics to the region near the event horizon, he found that black holes must emit thermal radiation and slowly evaporate. Virtual particle-antiparticle pairs form near the event horizon; occasionally one particle escapes while the other falls in. To a distant observer, the black hole appears to radiate. This was a landmark result because it combined general relativity, quantum mechanics, and thermodynamics — the three pillars of modern physics — for the first time in a single calculation.

Lecture 5 — The Origin and Fate of the Universe
Hawking presents the no-boundary proposal, developed with James Hartle. In ordinary physics, the Big Bang is a singularity — a point where the laws of physics break down and one must specify arbitrary initial conditions. The no-boundary proposal suggests that in imaginary time (a mathematical technique), the universe has no boundary or beginning. The universe is finite but has no edge, like the surface of a sphere: if you travel far enough in any direction, you come back to where you started. Asking what happened before the Big Bang is, on this view, as meaningless as asking what is south of the South Pole.

Lecture 6 — The Direction of Time
Why does time seem to flow in only one direction — from past to future? Hawking identifies three distinct "arrows of time":
1. The thermodynamic arrow: entropy (disorder) always increases. A cup falls and smashes; the smashed pieces do not spontaneously reassemble.
2. The psychological arrow: we remember the past but not the future.
3. The cosmological arrow: the universe is expanding, not contracting.
Hawking argues these arrows must all point in the same direction. We observe the thermodynamic and psychological arrows pointing the same way because intelligent beings can only exist during the expansion phase of the universe — during contraction, entropy would still be increasing relative to the contracting direction, but intelligent beings would perceive the arrows as reversed.

Lecture 7 — The Theory of Everything
The final lecture brings the quest together. Hawking surveys the candidate theories for a complete unification: supersymmetry, supergravity, and string theory. He explains that physicists hoped to find a single set of equations — perhaps no more than one line long — that would describe every particle and every force in the universe. He is cautiously optimistic but honest: we do not yet have such a theory. However, he argues that we are closer than ever. He famously ends with the observation that if we ever do find the Theory of Everything, "it would be the ultimate triumph of human reason — for then we would truly know the mind of God."

RELATIONSHIP TO HIS OTHER BOOKS

- "A Brief History of Time" (1988) covers similar ground but in greater depth and for a wider general audience. Theory of Everything is more concise, being based on lectures.
- "The Grand Design" (2010, with Leonard Mlodinow) is Hawking's most recent statement on the topic, arguing that M-theory (a version of string theory) is the best candidate for a Theory of Everything, and revisiting the question of whether God is necessary to explain the universe's existence.
- "A Briefer History of Time" (2005, with Leonard Mlodinow) is an updated and simplified version of A Brief History of Time.
- "Black Holes and Baby Universes" (1993) is a collection of essays including personal and autobiographical material.
- "Brief Answers to the Big Questions" (2018, published posthumously) addresses questions like "Is there a God?", "Will we survive on Earth?", and "Is time travel possible?"

HAWKING'S OWN VOICE ON THE THEORY OF EVERYTHING

Hawking spoke often and directly about his quest for unification. Key things he said:

"My goal is simple. It is complete understanding of the universe — why it is as it is, and why it exists at all."

"If we find a complete theory, it should in time be understandable in broad principle by everyone, not just a few scientists. Then we shall all, philosophers, scientists, and just ordinary people, be able to take part in the discussion of why it is that we and the universe exist."

"Up to now, most scientists have been too occupied with the development of new theories that describe what the universe is to ask the question why. On the other hand, the people whose business it is to ask why — the philosophers — have not been able to keep up with the advance of scientific theories. In the eighteenth century, philosophers considered the whole of human knowledge, including science, to be their field. But in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, science became too technical and mathematical for the philosophers. They have reduced the scope of their inquiries so much that Wittgenstein, the most famous philosopher of this century, said: 'The sole remaining task for philosophy is the analysis of language.' What a comedown from the great tradition of philosophy from Aristotle to Kant!"

"Even if there is only one possible unified theory, it is just a set of rules and equations. What is it that breathes fire into the equations and makes a universe for them to describe?"

ON THE 2014 FILM

The 2014 film "The Theory of Everything," directed by James Marsh and starring Eddie Redmayne as Hawking and Felicity Jones as Jane, is based on Jane Hawking's memoir about their life together. Hawking said the film was "broadly true" and that Redmayne "learned to think like me." He was deeply moved by the performance. The film won the Academy Award for Best Actor for Redmayne. Hawking had mixed feelings about the portrayal of his private life being made public, but appreciated the film's warmth and that it brought his work to new audiences.

SIGNIFICANCE

"The Theory of Everything" remains significant because it shows Hawking at his most accessible — distilling a lifetime of scientific inquiry into seven clear, human lectures. It is the closest thing to hearing Hawking explain, in his own words, not just what physics has discovered, but why it matters and where the grand quest for understanding leads. The title captures the ambition of his entire career: not just to describe the universe, but to understand it completely.
